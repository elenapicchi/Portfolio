Context

Ruby Cup's mission — and the gap

Ruby Cup is a social enterprise selling sustainable menstrual health products globally, operating a buy-one-give-one model: every product sold funds a donation to someone who cannot afford one. The environmental and social mission is embedded in the DNA of the business.

When I joined, the sustainability ambition was clear but not formalised. There was no external certification, no consolidated impact reporting, and no structured approach to communicating environmental outcomes to B2B partners. My role was to close that gap.

My Role

What I was responsible for

Sustainability Strategy Impact Reporting Carbon Neutral Certification Packaging Transformation B2B Corporate Partnerships Fundraising Stakeholder Communications

Approach

Three things I focused on

🌿

Formalise the sustainability credentials

Researched, selected and led the process to achieve Carbon Neutral certification — giving the business a recognised, externally verified sustainability standard to build credibility with partners and customers.

📦

Eliminate plastic from the supply chain

Led the transition from plastic packaging to 100% recycled paper and bio-based materials across the product line — a tangible, measurable reduction in environmental footprint.

📊

Make the impact visible and communicable

Produced the annual impact report and published articles on Ruby Cup's environmental outcomes — translating product sales data into meaningful environmental metrics for external audiences.

Impact Calculation

What the numbers mean

Each Ruby Cup saves up to 50kg of single-use plastic and 53kg of CO₂ over its lifetime compared to disposable alternatives. With 4,118 cups sold and donated in this period, that represents a potential 205,900kg of plastic avoided — the equivalent weight of roughly 170 cars.

These figures formed the backbone of our impact reporting and were used directly in B2B partner communications, helping corporate clients understand the measurable environmental contribution of their procurement decisions.

Corporate Partnerships

Building B2B relationships around impact

A key part of my work was building a corporate sales programme from scratch — targeting organisations with CSR and social procurement goals. This involved:

  • Evaluating prospective corporate partners against Ruby Cup's mission and values before engagement
  • Developing pitch materials that positioned Ruby Cup's impact data as the core value proposition
  • Managing relationships with enterprise clients focused on environmental and social impact procurement
  • Coordinating with the product team to ensure partner requirements were met without compromising sustainability standards

Fundraising

Connecting commercial work to broader advocacy

Beyond the core sustainability programme, I coordinated a fundraising initiative in partnership with Wellbeing of Women — raising £1,200 for women's health research. This gave Ruby Cup a tangible link between its commercial activity and the broader health and environmental outcomes it stands for.

Outcomes

What this work produced

  • Carbon Neutral certification achieved — first formal external sustainability credential for the business
  • 100% plastic-free packaging across the product line
  • Annual impact report produced and published, linking sales to environmental outcomes
  • B2B corporate partnerships programme built from zero, with enterprise clients acquired on CSR grounds
  • £1,200 raised for Wellbeing of Women research through coordinated fundraising campaign
  • 120% YoY revenue growth — demonstrating that sustainability and commercial performance are not in conflict

Reflection

What I learnt

Working inside a mission-led organisation taught me that impact is not something that happens alongside the work — it has to be designed into it. The most important decisions were not about marketing, but about which certifications to pursue, how to measure outcomes honestly, and how to communicate them in a way that held up to scrutiny from serious partners.

This experience is what drew me toward grant-making and programme work: I want to apply this kind of rigorous, values-led thinking to deciding which organisations and projects deserve support — and help them become more effective at achieving environmental change.
